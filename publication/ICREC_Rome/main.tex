% Abstract
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
  The carbon footprint of large language model (LLM) training has
  become a critical environmental concern, with single runs consuming
  millions of GPU-hours and emitting hundreds of tonnes of CO$_2$.
  Temporal workload shifting---pausing training during periods of
  high grid carbon intensity and resuming when the grid is
  cleaner---can reduce operational emissions, but existing approaches
  rely on ad-hoc thresholds without systematically exploring the
  policy design space. We present a simulation and optimization
  framework that, given a model's power profile and a region's
  historical carbon intensity time series, efficiently searches the
  three-dimensional policy space of pause threshold $\theta_p$,
  resume threshold $\theta_r$, and start date to identify
  Pareto-optimal configurations. The framework combines a
  discrete-event simulator replaying 5-minute-resolution Electricity
  Maps data (2022--2025) with an adaptive grid-search optimizer that
  converges within five to six iterations. We evaluate across two
  frontier models---DeepSeek~V3 (2048~GPUs) and Kimi~K2 (minimum
  256~GPU model-parallel group)---spanning five regions with diverse
  grid variability spectrum. Our central finding is that the optimal
  hysteresis margin ($\theta_p - \theta_r$) is consistently $\leq
  16$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh across every region and model: wide hysteresis
  degrades the savings-to-overhead ratio, making pausing effectively
  a binary clean/dirty decision. In high-variability grids (Germany,
  Italy), single-year savings reach 43.4\% and 32.7\% at 174 and
  199\% time overhead; multi-year averaging attenuates these to
  19.7\% and 18.7\%, indicating that averaging across multiple years
  provides no benefit and should therefore be avoided. Low-variance
  grids (China, United States) yield under 10\% savings regardless of
  policy. Our results provide practitioners with actionable,
  region-specific policies for carbon-aware LLM training.

  \keywords{Carbon-aware computing \and LLM training \and Hysteresis policy \and
    Grid carbon intensity \and Temporal workload shifting \and
  Simulation \and Sustainable AI.}
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
% 1. Introduction
% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

The energy consumption of large language model (LLM) training has
grown dramatically. Training a single frontier model requires
millions of GPU-hours across thousands of accelerators: DeepSeek~V3
consumed approximately 2.79 million GPU-hours on
2048~H800~GPUs~\cite{deepseek-ai_deepseek-v3_2024}, and Kimi~K2 was
trained on 15.5 trillion tokens (minimum 256~GPU model-parallel
group)~\cite{kimi_team_kimi_2025}. At a per-GPU power draw of 700~W
for an NVIDIA H800 GPU during training \cite{h800_specs} and a power
usage effectiveness (PUE) of 1.27 \cite{jegham_hungry_2025}, a single
run can emit hundreds of tonnes of CO$_2$ depending on the local grid
mix. As LLM deployment scales, managing this environmental cost
becomes essential for sustainable AI infrastructure.

Carbon-aware temporal workload shifting offers a promising mitigation
strategy. Grid carbon intensity fluctuates on timescales of minutes
to hours, driven by renewable generation, demand cycles, and
fossil-fuel dispatch. Pausing training when intensity exceeds a
threshold and resuming when it falls below a second threshold allows
a training run to preferentially consume clean energy without
hardware modification or algorithm changes. However, the policy
design space is non-trivial: the pause threshold $\theta_p$, resume
threshold $\theta_r$, and start date $t_s$ jointly determine the
cost, overhead, and carbon savings. Checkpoint overhead, idle power,
and grid statistical properties interact to shape the trade-off.

Prior work has established the viability of temporal workload
shifting for cloud batch jobs~\cite{wiesner_lets_2021}, demonstrated
geo-distributed training during renewable curtailment
windows~\cite{wiesner_distributed_2026}, and developed
datacenter-scale carbon frameworks~\cite{acun_carbon_2023}. The
emissions accounting methodology of
Patterson~et~al.~\cite{patterson_carbon_nodate} and regional
intensity data of Dodge~et~al.~\cite{dodge_measuring_2022} provide
essential foundations. Yet none of these works provides a systematic
method for {\em optimizing} the hysteresis policy parameters of a
single-site LLM training run. Ad-hoc thresholds predominate, and the
value of the hysteresis margin---the gap between pause and resume
thresholds---remains unexplored.

We address this gap with a discrete-event simulation engine and an
adaptive grid-search optimizer that together search the
three-dimensional policy space $(\theta_p, \theta_r, t_s)$ and return
Pareto-optimal configurations. The simulator replays historical
carbon intensity time series at 5-minute resolution, models the
energy consumption of training, checkpointing, and idle phases, and
evaluates candidate policies against a never-pause baseline. The
optimizer uses iterative zoom refinement to concentrate evaluations
in promising regions, converging within five to six iterations.
Together, they enable comprehensive policy space exploration without
the $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ cost of brute-force enumeration.

We evaluate across two state-of-the-art models (DeepSeek~V3 on 2048
  GPUs and Kimi~K2 (minimum 256~GPU model-parallel group), five
  electricity grid regions (Sweden, Germany, Italy, the United
  States, and China), and four years (2022--2025) of
  5-minute-resolution carbon intensity data. Across tens of thousands
  of simulation runs, we establish the first systematic Pareto
  frontiers for carbon-aware LLM training with hysteresis-based
  pausing. The results reveal a clear and actionable pattern: the
  optimal hysteresis margin is consistently narrow ($\leq
  16$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh), and wide hysteresis degrades the
  savings-to-overhead ratio in every region and model configuration.

  Our contributions are:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item A simulation framework for carbon-aware LLM training with
      hysteresis-based pausing, validated through structural monotonicity
      checks and cross-model scaling consistency.
    \item An adaptive grid-search optimizer that efficiently explores the
      $(\theta_p, \theta_r, t_s)$ policy space, converging within at most six
      iterations.
    \item A comprehensive empirical study covering two state-of-the-art models
      across five grid regions and multi-year replay, establishing systematic
      Pareto frontiers, to the best of our knowledge, for
      carbon-aware LLM training.
    \item An actionable design rule: the optimal hysteresis policy uses a
      narrow margin ($\theta_p - \theta_r \leq 16$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh). Wide
      hysteresis is never Pareto-optimal.
  \end{enumerate}

  \section{Background and Related Work}

  \paragraph{Carbon-aware temporal workload shifting.}
  Wiesner~et~al.~\cite{wiesner_lets_2021} demonstrated that delaying
  cloud batch jobs by a few hours to align with periods of low carbon
  intensity can reduce operational emissions by
  3--34\%\footnote{Experimental range in~\cite{wiesner_lets_2021}:
  nightly jobs 3--34\%, ML project scenarios 3--19\%.}, depending on
  job duration and regional grid profile. Their ``let's wait awhile''
  approach uses a pause-resume semantics similar to ours but targets
  generic cloud workloads rather than LLM training. Their findings
  establish a reference range against which our results can be validated.

  \paragraph{Carbon-aware distributed LLM training.}
  Extending temporal shifting to geo-distributed clusters,
  Wiesner~et~al.~\cite{wiesner_distributed_2026} trained a
  561M-parameter transformer during renewable curtailment windows
  across multiple data center sites, reducing operational emissions
  to 5--12\% of single-site baselines (i.e., 88--95\% savings). Our
  work is complementary: we focus on single-site policy optimization
  for frontier-scale models (671B+ parameters), where
  geo-distribution may be infeasible due to inter-node bandwidth constraints.

  \paragraph{Datacenter-scale carbon frameworks.}
  Acun~et~al.~\cite{acun_carbon_2023} developed Carbon Explorer, a
  holistic framework balancing operational and embodied carbon across
  hardware procurement and deployment. Their work operates at the
  datacenter planning timescale (years) rather than the
  per-training-run timescale (hours to days) that we target.

  \paragraph{Emissions accounting and measurement.}
  Patterson~et~al.~\cite{patterson_carbon_nodate} established the
  standard formula for attributing carbon emissions to ML training:
  GPU-hours multiplied by TDP, PUE, and regional grid carbon
  intensity. Dodge~et~al.~\cite{dodge_measuring_2022} extended this
  to cloud instances, showing that marginal rather than average grid
  intensity better captures emissions impact. Our simulation follows
  their methodology, using 5-minute-resolution marginal intensity data.

  \paragraph{Our distinction.}
  While each prior work advances carbon-aware computing, none
  provides a systematic policy parameter optimization framework for
  single-site LLM training. We contribute a characterization of the
  $(\theta_p, \theta_r, t_s)$ design space, a quantification of the
  hysteresis margin trade-off, and a practical optimization method
  applicable to any training configuration.

  \section{Problem Formulation}

  \subsection{System Model}

  We consider a single training job defined by: model parameter count $N$,
  dataset token count $T$, GPU count $G$, per-GPU training power $P_t$,
  per-GPU idle power $P_i$, facility PUE $\eta$, checkpoint save duration
  $\tau_c$, and checkpoint resume duration $\tau_r$. The job runs on a
  cluster in a single region whose grid carbon intensity $c(t)$ is a time
  series at 5-minute resolution.

  \subsection{Hysteresis Policy}

  A hysteresis policy is defined by two thresholds and a start date:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $\theta_p$: the pause threshold. When training and $c(t) > \theta_p$,
      training pauses, a checkpoint is saved, and GPUs enter idle state.
    \item $\theta_r$: the resume threshold. When paused and $c(t) \le \theta_r$,
      training resumes from the last checkpoint.
    \item $t_s$: the date and time at which training begins.
  \end{itemize}
  The policy is feasible only if $\theta_r \le \theta_p$; the
  difference $\Delta_\theta = \theta_p - \theta_r$ is the {\em
  hysteresis margin}. A margin of zero ($\theta_r = \theta_p$) means
  the job toggles at a single threshold; a positive margin introduces
  a dead zone where the job remains in its current state.

  \subsection{Objective}

  Savings and overhead are computed relative to a never-pause baseline:
  \begin{equation}
    S = \frac{E_b - E_p}{E_b} \times 100 \qquad
    O = \frac{T_p - T_b}{T_b} \times 100
  \end{equation}
  where $E_b, T_b$ are baseline energy and time, and $E_p, T_p$ are policy
  energy and time. We use a composite score that rewards carbon savings while
  enforcing an overhead budget $B$:
  \begin{equation}
    Q = \frac{\alpha \cdot S / 100 + 1 - (1 - \alpha) \cdot O / B}{2}
  \end{equation}
  The score is defined only for $O \le B$. With $\alpha = 1$ (used in all of our
  experiments), this simplifies to $Q = 1/2 + S/200$, making savings the sole
  objective while $B$ acts as a hard constraint. The never-pause baseline has
  $S = 0, O = 0, Q = 0.5$.

  \section{Simulation Framework}

  \subsection{Discrete-Event Simulator}

  We implement a discrete-event simulator that replays a training run under a
  given policy. At each 5-minute timestep $\Delta t$:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If training is active and $c(t) > \theta_p$, the job enters a
      checkpoint-pause cycle: it spends $\tau_c$ saving state at full power,
      then remains idle at $P_i$.
    \item If paused and $c(t) \le \theta_r$, the job resumes: it spends
      $\tau_r$ loading state (negligible with asynchronous checkpointing), then
      resumes training.
    \item Otherwise, the job continues in its current state.
  \end{itemize}
  Energy per timestep is $E(t) = G \cdot P \cdot \eta \cdot \Delta t$ where
  $P$ is $P_t$ when training and $P_i$ when paused. Emissions are $E(t) \cdot
  c(t)$. Total training tokens are tracked to ensure completion; pause
  episodes extend wall-clock duration proportionally.

  \subsection{Carbon Intensity Data}

  Carbon intensity data comes from the Electricity Maps API at
  5-minute resolution for five regions. Table~\ref{tab:grid}
  summarizes each region's mean intensity, standard deviation, and
  coefficient of variation (CV). The regions span a wide range:
  Sweden (clean, variable), Germany and Italy (moderately dirty,
  highly variable), and the United States and China
  (moderate-to-dirty, low variability).

  \subsection{Validation}

  The simulator is validated through structural monotonicity checks
  (higher $\theta_p$ yields lower overhead but lower savings;
    increasing the margin at fixed $\theta_p$ decreases savings without
  improving overhead), cross-model scaling consistency, and unit
  tests covering the pause-resume state machine and energy accounting.

  % 5. Optimization Method
  % ============================================================
  \section{Adaptive Grid-Search Optimization}

  Exhaustively enumerating the 3D policy space $(\theta_p, \theta_r, t_s)$ is
  computationally prohibitive: a naive grid at resolution $N$ per axis costs
  $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ where $D$ is the number of start-date samples. With
  $N=100$ and $D=52$, brute-force requires approximately 260,000 simulations.

  We instead use an adaptive grid-search algorithm inspired by trust-region
  optimization:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item {\bf Initialization.} A coarse grid over full bounds:
      $\theta_p \in [0, \theta_p^{\max}]$, $\theta_r \in [0, \theta_p]$, and
      $t_s$ at $D$ uniform intervals. The maximum $\theta_p^{\max}$ is
      region-dependent: 100~gCO$_2$eq/kWh for Sweden, 800 for all others.
    \item {\bf Evaluation and selection.} Every grid point is simulated. Points
      exceeding the overhead budget $B$ are discarded. From feasible
      points, the highest-scoring is selected.
    \item {\bf Zoom refinement.} Bounds contract around the best point by
      shrink factor $s = 0.45$ per axis. Grid resolution $N = 10$ points per
      axis per iteration remains fixed. If the contracted interval on any axis
      falls below $3$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh, search on that axis terminates.
    \item {\bf Iteration.} Steps 2--3 repeat for a maximum of $K = 10$
      iterations. The shrink factor $s=0.45$ ensures that after
      approximately six iterations the search window contracts below
      the minimum step of $3$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh.
  \end{enumerate}

  The algorithm guarantees a feasible point (the initial grid always contains
    the zero-hysteresis no-pause policy $\theta_p = \theta_p^{\max}, \theta_r =
  0$, yielding $0\%$ overhead). Total complexity is $O(K \cdot N^2 \cdot D)$;
  with our settings ($K=10, N=10, D=7$ for start-date experiments, $D=1$ for
  fixed-date runs), a full optimization evaluates approximately 3,500
  candidate policies (550 for fixed-start runs), completing in under one
  minute on a single CPU core. The optimizer converges within 5--6
  iterations in all (model, region) combinations; subsequent
  iterations yield no measurable improvement
  ($\Delta\text{score}\leq2\times10^{-5}$).

  \section{Experimental Setup}

  \paragraph{Models.}
  We evaluate DeepSeek~V3 (671B total parameters, 37B active with MoE,
  trained on 14.8T tokens using 2048~H800~GPUs, 2.79M~GPU-hours) and
  Kimi~K2 (1T parameters, 32B active with MoE, trained on 15.5T tokens,
  minimum 256~GPU model-parallel group). The GPU-hours for Kimi were
  estimated based on the GPU-hours per trillion tokens given by Deepseek.
  These represent the current frontier of LLM pretraining at
  substantially different scales.

  \paragraph{Hardware constants.}
  GPU training power: 700~W (NVIDIA H800 TDP~\cite{h800_specs}). GPU idle
  power: 60~W (estimated). PUE: 1.27 (DeepSeek DGX H800 infrastructure
  data~\cite{jegham_hungry_2025}). Checkpoint pause: 148.8~s (PyTorch
  Distributed synchronous checkpoint, 7B model~\cite{pytorch_checkpoint}).
  Checkpoint resume: 0~s (asynchronous checkpoint I/O).

  \paragraph{Regions and data.}
  Five regions shown in Table~\ref{tab:grid}. Four complete years (2022--2025) of
  5-minute-resolution marginal carbon intensity from Electricity Maps.

  \paragraph{Optimizer settings.}
  Resolution $N=10$, iterations $K=10$, budget $B=200\%$, shrink factor
  $s=0.45$, minimum step $3$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh, $\alpha=1.0$ (pure savings
  objective within budget). Start-date resolution: 1 sample (Jan~1) for
  fixed-date runs, 7 samples per year for start-date optimization.

  \section{Results}

  \subsection{Regional Grid Characterization}

  Grid variability---not mean intensity---determines savings potential.
  Germany (CV=0.37) and Italy (CV=0.28) offer the greatest temporal arbitrage
  opportunity because their carbon intensity oscillates between near-zero
  (renewable-rich) and high (fossil-dominated) values on hourly timescales.
  Sweden has high relative variability (CV=0.35) but such a low mean (23
  gCO$_2$eq/kWh) that the absolute emissions available to avoid are limited.
  The United States and China (CV=0.11) are consistently moderate and
  consistently dirty, respectively, offering few clean intervals worth waiting
  for.

  The threshold space analysis (example in Fig.~\ref{fig:threshold}) reveals the central
  finding: optimal hysteresis margins cluster near zero. Across all 10
  (model, region) combinations, the best-score points satisfy $\Delta_\theta
  \leq 16$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh, and in most cases $\Delta_\theta \leq
  10$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh (Table~\ref{tab:best}). Zero-hysteresis policies
  ($\theta_r = \theta_p$) achieve scores within 1--3\% of the optimum,
  confirming that a non-zero margin provides marginal practical benefit.

    }
    \label{fig:margin}

  Figure~\ref{fig:margin} shows the relationship between margin and score
  across all policies and zones for the Kimi~K2 model (with nearly identical results for DeepSeek~V3). Beyond $\Delta_\theta \approx
  50$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh, the score consistently plateaus or declines: wider
  margins accumulate idle time and checkpoint overhead without proportional
  additional savings. The intuition is straightforward: a wide margin means
  training resumes only at very low carbon intensities, causing the job to
  remain paused through many moderately clean intervals. The optimal policy
  treats carbon intensity as a binary clean/dirty signal rather than a
  multi-level threshold. Pausing is a binary clean/dirty decision, not a
  multi-level filter.

  \subsection{Start Date Sensitivity}

  Start date exerts a measurable but secondary influence. Across all
  (model, region) combinations, winter months (November--February) are
  disproportionately represented among optimal start dates, reflecting lower
  grid carbon intensity during the heating season. The effect ranges from
  approximately 5 percentage points of savings (Sweden) to under 2 percentage
  points (China), confirming that hysteresis threshold choice dominates
  start-date scheduling.

  Single-year results are point estimates reflecting that specific
  year's weather and grid conditions; multi-year analysis provides
  the expected range under typical conditions.

  A policy optimized on a single year may overfit to that year's specific
  weather patterns. Figure~\ref{fig:multiyear} and Table~\ref{tab:multiyear}
  compare single-year (2025) results against multi-year averages (2022--2025).
  The attenuation is substantial in high-variability regions: Germany drops
  from 43.4\% savings (score 0.717) to 19.7\% (score 0.599), and Italy drops
  from 32.7\% (score 0.663) to 18.7\% (score 0.594). Sweden, with more
  consistent inter-annual behavior, shows a smaller drop from 23.2\% to
  22.3\%.

  The attenuation has two causes. First, 2025 was anomalously clean in several
  European grids due to high renewable generation, creating favorable pausing
  conditions not representative of the longer-term average. Second, multi-year
  averaging smooths extreme events that make hysteresis-based pausing most
  effective. The practical implication is clear: policies should not
  be optimized
  on multi-year data to avoid the smoothing effect and ensure more
  realistic policies.

  \section{Discussion}

  \paragraph{Key findings.}
  This study establishes three principal results. First, the hysteresis margin
  should be near-zero: $\theta_r \approx \theta_p$. The intuitive appeal of a
  wide margin---resuming only when the grid is very clean---is undermined by
  idle time and checkpoint overhead. This finding is robust across two models,
  five regions, and four years of data, suggesting it is a fundamental
  property of the problem rather than an artifact of our settings.

  Second, regional grid profile---specifically temporal variance---dominates
  all other factors. The ranking of regions by savings potential (DE $>$ IT
  $>$ SE $>$ US $>$ CN) largely follows the ranking by coefficient of variation;
  mean intensity plays a secondary role---Sweden's high CV is offset by its
  very low mean (23~gCO$_2$eq/kWh), limiting the absolute emissions available
  to avoid. Practitioners in low-variance grids should not expect
  carbon-aware pausing to deliver meaningful savings and should consider
  alternative strategies.

  Third, start date exerts a secondary but consistent influence. Winter starts
  (November--February) outperform summer starts across all regions.

  \paragraph{Practical recommendations.}
  For high-variability grids (DE, IT): set $\theta_p$ at roughly
  20--40th percentile of regional carbon intensity
  (well below the mean), $\theta_r$ at $\theta_p - 5$ to
  $10$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh. Expect 30--43\% savings for DeepSeek~V3
  (17--27\% for Kimi~K2) at
  174--199\% overhead; multi-year averages yield 19--20\%. For
  clean-variable grids
  (SE): use tighter thresholds ($\theta_p \approx 20$--$30$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh)
  with near-zero hysteresis. Expect 12--23\% savings at 35--106\% overhead.
  For low-variance grids (CN, US): carbon-aware pausing yields under 10\%
  savings; consider geo-distributed training or embodied carbon trade-offs.
  Always optimize on a single year of data; multi-year optimization smooths
  the grid and yields less effective policies.

  \paragraph{Limitations.}
  Our simulation assumes perfect foresight: policies are evaluated on
  historical data rather than forecasted future intensity. A real deployment
  would require forecasting, introducing prediction error. We use a fixed
  checkpoint overhead (148.8~s); real overhead depends on model size and
  storage bandwidth. GPU idle power (60~W) is an estimate; actual values vary.
  Finally, this work considers single-site training; geo-distributed training
  could compound savings by adding spatial flexibility.

  \section{Conclusion}

  We have presented a simulation and optimization framework for carbon-aware
  LLM training via hysteresis-based pausing. The framework combines a
  discrete-event simulator with an adaptive grid-search optimizer to
  efficiently explore the three-dimensional policy space $(\theta_p,
  \theta_r, t_s)$. Applied to two state-of-the-art models across five grid
  regions and four years of data, it establishes systematic Pareto
  frontiers, to the best of our knowledge,
  for this problem.

  The central finding is that hysteresis provides diminishing returns: the
  optimal policy uses a narrow or zero margin ($\Delta_\theta \leq
  16$~gCO$_2$eq/kWh) in every region and model. Wide hysteresis margins waste
  time and checkpoint overhead without proportional savings. Carbon-aware
  pausing can reduce operational emissions by up to 43\% in high-variability
  grids at the cost of up to 200\% wall-clock overhead. In low-variance grids,
  savings fall below 10\%, and alternative mitigation strategies should be
  considered. Single-year optimization consistently captures higher savings:
  multi-year averaging attenuates extreme events and underestimates
  achievable savings by up to 2.4$\times$.

  Three directions are particularly promising for future work: (1) extending
  the framework to use forecasted rather than historical carbon intensity for
  real-time deployment; (2) combining temporal shifting with geo-distributed
  training; and (3) incorporating embodied carbon awareness to ensure
  operational savings are not outweighed by manufacturing emissions.

  All simulation code and data are available; our interactive
  browser-based simulator and optimizer can be tried at
  \url{https://404simon.github.io/TheGreenEpoch}.

  \paragraph{Broader impact.}
  As LLM training scales, carbon-aware scheduling can serve as
  demand-side flexibility for renewable energy grid integration:
  pausing during high-carbon periods reduces reliance on fossil-fuel
  peaker plants and aligns compute demand with renewable supply. This
  synergy is particularly relevant for grid operators managing
  increasing penetrations of variable renewable generation.
